01 / THE PROBLEM

The Friction of Literacy

Classic literature is inherently demanding. When the digital medium adds "chaotic menus" and "confusing filters" to an already difficult task, users simply give up. I found that even with a strong need for accessible classics, the current friction was discouraging users from the books themselves.

Fig 01. Analyzing the navigation debt in the original application.

02 / RESEARCH FINDINGS

Design as a Trust Signal

First impressions are 94% influenced by design. I audited the current state and discovered a staggering disconnect: 80% of users had a negative first reaction. The problem wasn't the content, but the vessel carrying it.

I.

Color Overload

Too many competing colors distracted from the reading experience.

II.

Legibility

Typography failed to meet the standards required for long-form reading.

III.

Feature Fog

Users were confused by basic features, such as the "Patron" system.

"75% of users are more likely to trust a visually appealing website—52% won't return if aesthetics fail."
03 / USER PERSONA

User Persona: The Time-Poor Student

Research confirmed that while avid readers use the app, the primary pain points are felt most acutely by students like Maciek. For him, the app isn't just a library; it's a tool that needs to compete with a busy work-study schedule.

Maciek, 17

Student Unmotivated Busy

"I have 15 minutes to find a reference—if the app is a maze, I’m out."

Primary Need

A streamlined reading dashboard that prioritizes Progress Tracking and Instant Library Access to maximize limited study windows.

04 / DESIGN STRATEGY

The Iterative Loop

I initially sketched solutions that stayed close to the original layout to avoid "re-learning" costs for existing users. Although users liked some of the updated features, there were still problems to solve.

Iteration 01: Feedback was harsh but necessary—"Button names are unclear," and "The scroll is endless."

Applying Miller's Law

To reduce Cognitive Load, I implemented Progressive Disclosure. By truncating filters and only showing secondary information when requested, I allowed Maciek to focus on one decision at a time.

Before

Chaotic filters leading to 'Analysis Paralysis'.

After

Clean symmetry and truncated categories.

05 / IMPACT

Impact & Conclusion

Structure creates trust. I stripped away the visual clutter of the original design to prioritize functional clarity. This shift didn't just organize the app; it empowered users to actually utilize the features they once ignored due to poor labeling and layout chaos.

Takeaway: Adhering to design principles is essential, but it is no substitute for deep research. Through this process, I learned that even the most 'correct' UI can fail if it doesn't align with the user's core goals. Sometimes the solution isn't a better button—it’s a new functionality altogether. UX research doesn't just polish an app; it defines the roadmap for what should be built next.
